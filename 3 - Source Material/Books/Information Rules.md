## Information

Intellectual goods or information is also a commodity that can be sold. It's very costly to produce and assemble, but the cost structure of an information supplier is very unusual. Since the very nature of competition in information markets is driven by this unusual cost structure, we begin our overview of information strategy there.

## The cost of producing information

Information is costly to produce but cheap to reproduce. This leads to pricing based on the value to consumer rather than price to produce. The marginal costs of the product is so low that a 20% or 30% increase in the price makes no sense, so cost based pricing just doesn't work. Since people have varied sense of value to a particular information, value based pricing leads to naturally leads to differential pricing.

This can be done in multiple ways, once way publishers do this is by only selling the expensive hardback version of the book first and then the paper back versions. Providers of information on the internet can exploit the same strategy: investors now pay $8.95 a month for a Web site that offers portfolio analysis using 20- minute delayed stock market quotes but $50 a month for a service that uses real-time stock market quotes.

## Managing Intellectual Property

If the creators of an information good can reproduce it cheaply, others can copy it cheaply. It has long been recognized that some form of "privatization" of information helps to ensure its production. The U.S. Constitution explicitly grants Congress the duty "to promote the progress of science and useful arts, by securing, for limited times, to authors and inventors, the exclusive right to their respective writings and discoveries.

But the legal grant of exclusive rights it not enough, with the rise of technology piracy is now easier than ever. If copies crowd out legitimate sales, the producers of information may not be able to recover their productions costs.

## Information as an "Experience Good"

Economists say that a good is an experience good if consumers must consume it to value it. That makes information an experience good every time its consumed. There are various strategies to get wary customers to overcome their reluctance to purchase information before they know what they're getting, through providing attracting samples to building a brand reputation.

>The tension between giving away your information—to let people know what you have to offer—and charging them for it to recover your costs is a fundamental problem in the information economy.
