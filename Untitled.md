Lately, mornings haven’t been very kind to me—this week is packed with events to organize and quizzes to pass. Usually I'm the one to wish people good morning, but when I looked over to my phone, I noticed a bunch of good morning texts from my friends and family. My sister likes adding the "chotu" to it just 'cause I'm younger, my dad doesn't add anything and leaves it simple and plain, my girlfriend likes following it up with something cute. My roommate, who's still lying on his bed doom scrolling notices I'm up, "aaj bhi late utha?" he says. I reply with a heavy sigh, wash up and get on with my day. I check my emails in-case anything important had come up, then I open Instagram, some friends have shared a few reels - memes, motivational, cute couple stuff - that I watch while I'm on my way to lunch. I pass by a few people I know, a quick nod to one, a smile to another, a "bhai, essay chalu kiya?" to a friend who definitely didn't start yet. During lunch, I realize my mom hasn't texted me yet. I chuckle—she always waits for me to message first, as if it’s her way of making sure I’m finally awake.

It's lunch and I've already had five different types of conversation—some verbal, some not. Thinking about it, I realize how distinct they are from one another—not just in form or medium, but in the way they reflect relationships. A simple text message can mean different things depending on who it’s from. Close ones like to personalize their messages, while friends from home keep it short and casual. My roommate, who knows me well, doesn't bother with morning greetings at all. Emails, to me, are the most formal way to communicate—one misplaced informal phrase, and it could hurt your reputation. On the other hand, reels or shorts—different names, same thing—have become a modern way to express emotions without words. It could be a shared laugh or a short that might subtly say, *I miss you*. Non-verbal communication often speaks louder than words; it shows the mood of a relationship. A nod might be all that's left of an old friendship, or it could be an unspoken understanding between two people whose friend groups don't align but who still share a bond.
